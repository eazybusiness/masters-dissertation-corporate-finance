## 2.3 Capital Structure and Firm Value: Reassessing the Trade-Off Framework in the Post-Crisis Era

The question of how firms choose between debt and equity financing — and whether that choice affects firm value — has occupied financial economists for more than six decades. This subchapter traces the theoretical evolution from the capital-structure irrelevance proposition to the modern trade-off and pecking-order frameworks, before examining recent empirical evidence that challenges the predictive power of both.

### 2.3.1 The Modigliani–Miller Foundation

Any rigorous treatment of capital structure must begin with Modigliani and Miller's (1958) irrelevance theorem, which demonstrated that, under perfect capital markets, the market value of a firm is independent of its debt–equity mix. The proposition rests on the assumptions of no taxes, no bankruptcy costs, symmetric information, and frictionless trading. While these conditions are plainly unrealistic, the theorem's analytical value lies precisely in identifying which market imperfections make capital structure relevant (Myers, 2001).

Modigliani and Miller (1963) themselves relaxed the no-tax assumption, showing that the tax deductibility of interest payments creates a debt tax shield whose present value increases linearly with leverage. Taken literally, this correction implies that firms should finance almost entirely with debt — a prediction clearly at odds with observed leverage ratios. The resolution of this paradox motivated the two dominant frameworks that followed.

### 2.3.2 The Static Trade-Off Theory

The static trade-off theory, formalised by Kraus and Litzenberger (1973), posits that firms select an optimal capital structure by balancing the marginal benefit of the debt tax shield against the marginal cost of financial distress. At low leverage, the tax advantage dominates; beyond a threshold, expected bankruptcy costs — both direct (legal fees, asset fire-sales) and indirect (loss of customers, suppliers, and key employees) — erode the tax benefit, producing a concave relationship between leverage and firm value.

Empirical support for the trade-off framework is mixed. Graham (2000) estimated that the typical large US corporation exploits only about 50 per cent of its potential debt tax shield, leaving substantial value on the table if the model's logic held without qualification. Frank and Goyal (2009) conducted a comprehensive factor analysis and found that profitability, firm size, tangibility of assets, and industry median leverage are the most reliably significant determinants of capital structure — results broadly consistent with trade-off reasoning, yet insufficient to explain the considerable heterogeneity in leverage across otherwise similar firms.

A further complication arises from the empirical observation that highly profitable firms tend to carry less debt, not more (Rajan and Zingales, 1995). Under the trade-off theory, profitable firms should borrow more because they have larger taxable income to shield and face lower distress risk. The inverse relationship between profitability and leverage thus constitutes a persistent anomaly for the static trade-off model.

### 2.3.3 The Pecking-Order Theory

Myers and Majluf (1984) offered an alternative explanation rooted in information asymmetry. Because managers possess superior information about the firm's prospects, external investors interpret equity issuance as a signal that shares are overvalued. Rational managers therefore prefer internal funds first, then debt, and issue equity only as a last resort — a financing hierarchy Myers (1984) termed the "pecking order."

The pecking-order theory neatly explains the negative profitability–leverage relationship: firms with high earnings generate sufficient retained cash flow to avoid external financing altogether. However, the theory struggles with other stylised facts. Fama and French (2005) documented that equity issuance is far more common than the pecking order predicts, particularly among small, high-growth firms. Leary and Roberts (2010) further showed that firms frequently deviate from the strict financing hierarchy, suggesting that information-asymmetry costs alone cannot account for observed behaviour.

### 2.3.4 Post-Crisis Developments and the Role of Financial Flexibility

The 2007–2009 global financial crisis exposed the limitations of both frameworks in a setting where credit markets froze and leverage suddenly became a survival variable rather than an optimisation parameter. DeAngelo and DeAngelo (2007) had already argued that financial flexibility — the ability to access capital markets at low cost when profitable investment opportunities arise — should be recognised as a first-order determinant of capital structure. Their argument gained considerable traction after the crisis, as firms with conservative pre-crisis leverage weathered the downturn significantly better than highly leveraged peers (Fahlenbrach, Prilmeier and Stulz, 2012).

Graham and Leary (2011), in their authoritative review, concluded that neither the trade-off nor the pecking-order theory alone provides a satisfactory account of capital-structure decisions. Instead, they advocate integrating insights from both, alongside managerial behavioural factors such as CEO overconfidence (Malmendier, Tate and Yan, 2011) and market-timing incentives (Baker and Wurgler, 2002). This eclectic perspective acknowledges that capital-structure choices are shaped by a constellation of tax incentives, distress costs, information frictions, agency conflicts, and managerial characteristics — none of which can be reduced to a single optimising rule.

### 2.3.5 Implications for the Present Study

The literature reviewed in this subchapter carries two implications for the research design adopted in subsequent chapters. First, any empirical model of capital-structure determinants must control for profitability, asset tangibility, growth opportunities, and industry effects simultaneously, as omitting any one factor risks biased coefficient estimates (Frank and Goyal, 2009). Second, the post-crisis emphasis on financial flexibility suggests that static leverage ratios may be an inadequate dependent variable; dynamic measures capturing the speed of adjustment toward target leverage may yield richer insights (Flannery and Rangan, 2006).
